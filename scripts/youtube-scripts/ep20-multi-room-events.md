# Episode 20 — Managing Multi-Room & Multi-Track Events
**Duration target:** 5–6 min
**Style:** Structured and confident — this is an advanced episode for experienced production teams. The viewer has already run single-room events; now they're scaling up. Use a concrete 3-track conference as the running example throughout: "Main Stage", "Workshop A", "Workshop B". Anchor sessions (plenary moments that hold the whole programme) are a key concept — explain the mental model clearly before showing the mechanic.

>
> Now let's scale it up. In this episode we're running a three-track conference — a main plenary stage and two parallel workshop rooms running simultaneously. This is where CueDeck's multi-room features come in.

## ADDING ROOMS TO SESSIONS (0:35–1:30)

[Screen: Open the session edit modal for one session. The Room field is visible — a text input. Type "Main Stage" and save.]

> "Every session in CueDeck has a Room field. It's a free-text input — you type the room name and save it. There's no separate room setup required; the room names are generated by whatever you type.
>
> For a three-track event, I'd use consistent names across all sessions: 'Main Stage', 'Workshop A', 'Workshop B'. Capitalisation and spacing matter here — 'Workshop A' and 'workshop a' would show as two separate rooms in the filter bar, so pick a format and stick with it across your whole programme."

[Screen: Open a second session — show the Room field already suggesting "Main Stage" or pre-populated. Open a third session and show "Workshop A" being entered.]

> "Once you've set rooms on your sessions, the filter bar at the top of the session list automatically generates a chip for each unique room name. You don't configure this anywhere — it builds itself from the data.
>
> With three rooms, you'll see three room chips in the filter bar. Click one to isolate that room. Click it again to clear the filter and see everything."

---

## THE ROOM FILTER CHIPS (1:30–2:15)

[Screen: The filter bar is visible at the top of the session list. Three chips are visible: "Main Stage", "Workshop A", "Workshop B". Click "Workshop A".]

> "Click Workshop A — the session list immediately shows only Workshop A sessions. Everything else is hidden.
>
> This is the primary tool for a multi-room event. Each stage manager running their own room filters to their room and ignores the rest. The director can see everything or filter as needed.
>
> The filter works alongside the status filter and the search bar. So you can filter to Workshop A AND only READY or LIVE sessions at the same time. In a large event with thirty or forty sessions across five rooms, that combination is essential."

[Screen: With Workshop A filter active, also click the LIVE status chip — only the live Workshop A session remains visible.]

> "Click another room chip to switch to that room. Click the active chip to deselect it. The filter state is persisted through the current session — close and reopen the context panel and your filter stays active."

[Screen: Click "Workshop A" again to deselect — full session list returns. Show all three room chips, unselected.]

## ASSIGNING ROLES PER ROOM (2:15–2:50)

[Screen: Show the team/operators panel or the role management view — a list of operators and their roles.]

> "For a multi-room event, I typically structure the team by room. The director has the full view — all three rooms, all sessions. Each stage manager is assigned to one room and filters their view to that room from the start of the day. They see only what they need to see and act only on their sessions.
>
> The director broadcasts messages to everyone, monitors the full programme from above, and handles cross-room decisions like delay cascades. The stage managers handle the ground-level transitions for their room.
>
> This structure scales cleanly. Adding a fourth room means inviting one more stage manager and telling them their room name — no additional setup required."

---

## ANCHOR SESSIONS (2:50–3:50)

[Screen: Open a session edit modal. Scroll to the Anchor Session toggle — a checkbox labelled "Anchor Session (is_anchor)". Enable it. Save.]

> "Now for one of the most important concepts in multi-room delay management: anchor sessions.
>
> An anchor session is a plenary moment — lunch break, networking session, closing keynote, registration — that affects every room. When the main stage runs long and you apply a delay to Workshop A, the delay cascade rolls forward through that room's subsequent sessions. But it stops at the next anchor session.
>
> The anchor holds its position. It doesn't shift. Because lunch starts at thirteen hundred whether Workshop A's morning panel ran six minutes over or not. The catering team, the hotel, the signage in the lobby — all of that is fixed to the anchor time."

[Screen: Show a delay being applied to a Workshop B session. The delay cascade fires — sessions below it in Workshop B shift. Then show an anchor session (e.g. Networking Lunch) that does NOT shift — it stays at its original time.]

> "Look at what happens when I apply a five-minute delay to this Workshop B session. The sessions below it in Workshop B shift forward by five minutes — that's the cascade doing its job. But the Networking Lunch session, which is marked as an anchor, stays exactly where it is. It does not absorb the delay.
>
> The practical implication: your workshop sessions will need to compress slightly before the anchor, or you accept that the final pre-lunch session ends a bit late. That's a call for the director, and CueDeck gives you the information to make it in real time."

[Screen: Show the anchor session with a distinctive visual indicator — a small lock or anchor icon on the card.]

> "Anchor sessions have a visual marker on their card — a small indicator that tells you at a glance 'this one doesn't move'. Scan the session list and you can immediately see which sessions are the fixed points of your programme."

---

## PROGRAMME SIGNAGE MODE (3:50–4:45)

[Screen: Navigate to the Signage panel in the sidebar. Select a display. Change its mode to "Programme". The Programme display preview appears — a time-versus-room grid: rows are time slots, columns are room names (Main Stage, Workshop A, Workshop B). Sessions appear in the correct cell.]

> "The Programme display mode is the master view for a multi-track event. It renders a time-by-room grid — rows running down the left are time slots, columns across the top are your room names. Every session appears in the correct cell at the correct time.
>
> Put this on a large screen in the main lobby or the registration area and attendees can see the entire day's programme at a glance — every track, every room, every time slot, all in one view. As sessions go live they update in real time. A session that's running five minutes late will show its updated time if you've applied a delay cascade."

[Screen: Show the Programme display mode on a signage screen — the grid rendering with sessions in their correct cells. Then show a delay being applied and the session time updating in the grid live.]

> "The Programme mode reads from the same session data as the console. There's no separate configuration for the grid — it builds from your session room names and scheduled times automatically. Add a room by assigning sessions to a new room name, and the column appears in the Programme display."

---

## PRACTICAL TIPS FOR A 3-TRACK CONFERENCE (4:45–5:20)

[Screen: Full director view, all three rooms visible in the session list, interleaved. No filter active.]

> "Three practical things I've learned from running multi-room events on CueDeck.
>
> First: set your anchor sessions before the event starts. Identify all the plenary moments — lunch, networking breaks, the closing keynote — and mark them as anchors in the session edit modal. This takes five minutes and saves you from accidentally cascading a delay into a fixed point.
>
> Second: use room naming conventions your whole team knows before show day. Brief everyone: 'our rooms are Main Stage, Workshop A, Workshop B.' One naming convention, communicated in advance.
>
> Third: for delay decisions, the director filters to the affected room, sees the cascade, decides if it's acceptable or needs a broadcast to attendees. That decision should happen within sixty seconds of the overrun starting — the delay cascade makes the impact visible immediately."
