# Episode 13 — AI Post-Event Report Generator
**Duration target:** 5–6 min
**Style:** Professional, reflective; the event is over and now we're in the debrief phase; tone shifts from the urgency of live production to the clarity of documentation; this is the "exhale" episode

---

## INTRO (0:00–0:35)

[Screen: CueDeck console, session list — all sessions in ENDED state; the clock shows late afternoon]

> "The last session has ended. The speakers are packing up. The room is clearing out. And somewhere in the next 48 hours, you need to send your client a debrief — how the day ran, how close you were to schedule, what incidents happened and how they were handled.
>
> Normally that document takes two hours. You're pulling times from your notes, cross-referencing with the programme, trying to remember which AV issue happened in which room and what the fix was.
>
> The CueDeck Report Generator does all of that in one click. Let me show you."

---

## WHERE TO FIND IT (0:35–1:05)

[Screen: pan to the AI Agents panel in the sidebar — show the three module cards: Incident Advisor, Cue Engine, Report Generator; click the Report Generator card]

> "The Report Generator is the third AI agent module, in the AI Agents panel. It's director-only — same as the other two agents — because this is a document that will go to your client or your internal team.
>
> Click Generate Report and the process starts immediately. CueDeck pulls all the data it needs from the current event — session records, scheduled versus actual times, the incident log, everything that happened during the day — and passes it to Claude to generate the report."

---

## THE THINKING ANIMATION (1:05–1:35)

[Screen: the report modal is open — blue accent bar at top; in the body, a large spinner with five animated step labels cycling through: "Analyzing session data" → "Processing incident log" → "Calculating performance metrics" → "Drafting executive summary" → "Writing recommendations"]

> "While the report generates you'll see this thinking animation — five steps cycling through, each one lighting up as the AI works through that phase of the report. Session analysis, incident processing, metric calculation, summary drafting, recommendations.
>
> This typically takes fifteen to twenty seconds. When it finishes, the tabs appear and the content is ready."

---

## THE SUMMARY TAB (1:35–2:30)

[Screen: the Summary tab is active; show four stat cards at the top of the modal — "14 Sessions", "86% On Time", "+4.2 min avg overrun", "2 Incidents"; below the stats, two text sections: Executive Summary and Technical Performance]

> "The first tab is Summary — and this is what I'd open if I were sending something to a client.
>
> At the top, four stat cards: total sessions run today, the on-time rate as a percentage, the average overrun across all sessions, and the total number of incidents logged. These are pulled from your actual session data — not estimates.
>
> Below that, two written sections generated by Claude: an Executive Summary — three to four sentences, professional tone, suitable for a client email — and a Technical Performance paragraph covering AV system reliability, streaming stability, and crew execution.
>
> This is the kind of language that normally takes thirty minutes to write. It's already there, it's accurate, and it's based on what actually happened today."

---

## THE SESSIONS TAB (2:30–3:15)

[Screen: click the Sessions tab; a table appears — columns: Session Title, Room, Planned Start, Actual Start, Delta; rows for each session; delta values are green for on-time, orange for slight overrun, red for major overrun]

> "The Sessions tab is your variance table. Every session, side by side: planned start time versus actual start time, and the delta in minutes.
>
> Green deltas mean on time or early. Orange means a minor overrun — within a few minutes. Red means it ran significantly late. You can see at a glance where the day was tight and where it slipped.
>
> This table is what you'd use for your internal debrief — understanding which rooms ran well, which sessions need buffer time added next year, whether the lunch break was long enough. It's also useful for invoice justification if you're billing for overtime on a specific room."

---

## THE INCIDENTS TAB (3:15–3:55)

[Screen: click the Incidents tab; a table shows — columns: Time, System, Location, Description, Status; each row has a coloured badge: RESOLVED (green), ESCALATED (red); one or two example rows visible]

> "The Incidents tab is the log of everything that was flagged through the Incident Advisor during the day. Each entry shows the time it was triggered, the system affected, the location, the description you typed when you raised it, and the final status — resolved or escalated.
>
> If you had two incidents during the day and both were resolved on-site, this tab tells that story clearly. If one was escalated to a senior technician, that's logged here too.
>
> For a client-facing debrief, this is the section you use to explain what went wrong and what was done about it. Transparent, timestamped, factual."

---

## THE RECOMMENDATIONS TAB (3:55–4:40)

[Screen: click the Recommendations tab (labelled as the fourth tab in the modal); a section header reads "AI NARRATIVE" in small caps above a full paragraph of written text, followed by a numbered list of recommendations]

> "The fourth tab is where the report really earns its place. This is the AI Narrative — a full written debrief generated by Claude, covering how the day ran in plain professional English.
>
> It synthesises everything: the session count, the schedule adherence, the incidents, the technical environment, and the crew's execution. The result reads like something written by a senior production manager reflecting on the event — because in a sense, that's exactly what it is.
>
> Below the narrative, a numbered recommendations list. Four to five items, specific and actionable: add buffer time before the closing keynote, assign a dedicated stream monitor for events above five hundred online attendees, confirm EDID compatibility for the main stage switcher before doors open. Real recommendations based on what actually happened today."

---

## EXPORTING THE REPORT (4:40–5:15)

[Screen: show the footer of the modal — "COPY TEXT" button on the left, "PRINT / PDF" button, "CLOSE" button; click "COPY TEXT" and show a brief confirmation; then click "PRINT / PDF" and describe what happens]

> "Once you're happy with the report, two export options are in the footer.
>
> Copy Text puts the full report onto your clipboard as plain text — ready to paste into an email, a Google Doc, or a Notion page.
>
> Print / PDF opens the browser print dialog, formatted for print. From there you can save as a PDF and attach it to your client debrief, or print it for your own records.
>
> The report includes the event name, date, and a generated timestamp — so it's clearly identified and ready to send."

---

## USE CASES (5:15–5:45)

[Screen: return to the closed modal, session list all ENDED, a clean end-of-day view]

> "Three main use cases for this.
>
> Client debrief: copy the Executive Summary and the Incidents log and send it within the hour. Clients appreciate fast, professional follow-through.
>
> Internal review: use the Sessions variance table in your next production planning meeting. The overruns tell you where to add buffer in the schedule next time.
>
> Invoice justification: if you're billing for additional crew time or technical callouts, the incident log and timestamps give you the documentation to back it up.
>
> One click. Full report. Ready to send."
